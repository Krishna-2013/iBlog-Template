Diwali - The Festival of Lights

Celebrating Light Over Darkness

Author - Krishna

Date - 15th February, 2026 (1 minute to read)

Introduction

Diwali, also known as the Festival of Lights, is one of the most important and widely celebrated festivals in India. It symbolizes the victory of light over darkness, good over evil, and knowledge over ignorance. The festival usually falls between October and November and is celebrated with great enthusiasm by millions of people.

Historical and Religious Significance

Diwali holds deep religious meaning in Hindu tradition. It is associated with the return of Lord Rama to Ayodhya after defeating Ravana. People welcomed him by lighting oil lamps, which is why lighting lamps remains a central tradition.

The festival is also significant for other communities. It marks the worship of Goddess Lakshmi, the goddess of wealth and prosperity. For many businesses, Diwali marks the beginning of a new financial year.

Celebrations and Traditions

Homes are cleaned and decorated with rangoli designs and oil lamps called diyas. Families gather to perform prayers, exchange gifts, and share sweets. Fireworks are often used to express joy, although many people now prefer eco-friendly celebrations.

New clothes, festive meals, and visiting friends and relatives are common practices during the festival.

Modern Perspective

While Diwali is rooted in tradition, modern celebrations emphasize safety and environmental awareness. Many communities encourage reducing noise and air pollution by limiting fireworks and promoting sustainable decorations.

Conclusion

Diwali is more than just a festival; it is a reminder to remove negativity from life and move toward positivity and enlightenment. Its message of hope, unity, and renewal continues to inspire people across generations.
